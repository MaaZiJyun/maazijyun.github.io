5. Event Relations

The union of two events, A and B, is the event that either A or B or both (all colour parts) occur when the experiment is performed. We write A ∪ B

The intersection of two events, A and B, is the event that both A and B (green part) occur when the experiment is performed. We write A ∩ B.

*If two events A and B are mutually exclusive (only one result can happen), then P(A ∩ B) = 0.

The complement of an event A consists of all outcomes of the experiment that do not result in event A. We write ∁uA

Calculating Probabilities for Unions and Complements

The Additive Rule for Unions: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

example:

Select a student from the classroom and record his/her hair colour and gender.

-A: student has brown hair

-B: student is female

-C: student is male

Suppose that there were 120 students in the classroom, and that they could be classified as follows:

A: brown hair

P(A) = 50/120

B: female

P(B) = 60/120

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

= 50/120 + 60/120 - 30/120

= 80/120 = 2/3

(Special situation)

When two events A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A ∩ B) = 0

and P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B).

A: male with brown hair

P(A) = 20/120

B: female with brown hair

P(B) = 30/120

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - 0

= 20/120 + 30/120

= 50/120

Calculating Probabilities for Complements

P(A∁) = 1 – P(A)

Select a student at random from the classroom. Define:

A: male

P(A) = 60/120

B: female

P(B) = 1 - P(A)

= 1- 60/120 = 60/120

Calculating Probabilities for Intersections

the different between P(AB) and P(B|A)

Two events A and B are independent if and only if

P(A|B) = P(A) or P(B|A) = P(B)

Otherwise, they are dependent.

For any two events, A and B, the probability that both A and B occur is

P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B|A)

If the events A and B are independent, then the probability that both A and B occur is

P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B)

example:

In a certain population, 10% of the people can be classified as being high risk for a heart attack. Three people are randomly selected from this population. What is the probability that exactly one of the three are high risk?

Define H: high risk N: not high risk

P(H) = 0.1

P(N) = 1 - P(H) = 0.9

P(exactly one high risk) = P(HNN) + P(NHN) + P(NNH)

= P(H)P(N)P(N) + P(N)P(H)P(N) + P(N)P(N)P(H)

= (.1)(.9)(.9) + (.9)(.1)(.9) + (.9)(.9)(.1)= 3(.1)(.9)2

= .243

Suppose we have additional information in the previous example. We know that only 49% of the population are female. Also, of the female patients, 8% are high risk. A single person is selected at random. What is the probability that it is a high risk female?

Define H: high risk F: female

P(F) = 0.49

P(H|F) = 0.08

Use the Multiplicative Rule:

P(high risk female) = P(H ∩ F)

= P(F)P(H|F) =.49(.08) = .0392
